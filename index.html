Top Signs Your Sash Windows Are Letting Heat Escape

If you’ve noticed your home is colder than expected despite heating, your sash windows may be to blame. Traditional timber sash windows are elegant but often poorly sealed by today’s standards. This can lead to unwanted draughts and significant heat loss. Knowing what to look for can help you take the right steps to fix the problem and improve the energy performance of your home.

1. You Feel Cold Draughts Near the Window

This is the most obvious sign of heat escaping. If you can feel a flow of cold air when standing near the window, especially around the meeting rail or sill, it's likely there are gaps letting warm air out and cold air in. Older sash windows with worn-out seals or ill-fitting sashes are often the culprits.

2. Curtains Move on Windy Days (Even When Windows Are Shut)

Movement in curtains or blinds during windy conditions is a tell-tale sign of air leakage. It means outdoor air is entering through gaps around the sash frame, displacing warm indoor air. This also suggests heat is being lost unnecessarily and your heating system is working overtime to maintain temperature.

3. Windows Rattle or Shake

Rattling windows suggest the sash panes are not properly secured within the frame. This not only allows for noise penetration but also indicates weak insulation. Loose frames create space for warm air to escape, especially around the edges.

4. High Heating Bills

If you’ve ruled out boiler or radiator issues but your energy bills are still higher than they should be, your windows may be the reason. Heat lost through gaps in timber sash windows increases the demand on your heating system, leading to more energy consumption and higher costs.

5. Mould or Condensation Near the Frame

Moisture near the bottom rail or along the internal window ledge can be caused by uneven temperatures and poor ventilation. If warm indoor air escapes and hits a cold window surface, condensation forms. This not only signals heat loss but may lead to further issues like rot and paint damage.

6. Visible Gaps in the Frame or Joinery

Inspect your windows closely. If you can see daylight around the edge of the sash, especially where the two sashes meet or near the cords, heat is escaping. Period homes with timber frames often shift over time, creating these unintended openings.

What You Can Do About It

The best solution is draught proofing. This involves sealing the gaps around moving parts using modern brush seals that remain hidden but highly effective. Draught proofing improves warmth, reduces energy bills, and enhances comfort without compromising the heritage value of the window.

Secondary glazing and, where permitted, slim-profile double glazing can also help improve insulation. These measures are designed to be sympathetic to period properties and often qualify for use in conservation areas when installed properly.

Final Thoughts

If your sash windows are showing any of these signs, it’s time to act. Early intervention with draught proofing or careful repair not only keeps your home warmer but also prevents more costly repairs later down the line.
